\section{Dynamic Analysis using Euler-Lagrange Formulation}

This section derives the complete equations of motion for the hybrid serial-cable driven planar robot using the Euler-Lagrange method. This formulation provides a systematic approach to dynamics that naturally incorporates constraints and is particularly suited for systems with mixed actuators.

\subsection{Euler-Lagrange Method Foundation}

The Euler-Lagrange equation for a mechanical system with generalized coordinates $\mathbf{q}$ is:

\begin{equation}
\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{\mathbf{q}}}\right) - \frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{q}} = \mathbf{Q}
\end{equation}

where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $L = T - V$ is the Lagrangian (Kinetic energy minus Potential energy)
    \item $T$ is the total kinetic energy
    \item $V$ is the total potential energy
    \item $\mathbf{Q}$ are the generalized forces/torques not derivable from potential energy
    \item $\mathbf{q} = [q_1, q_2]^T$ are the joint angles (generalized coordinates)
\end{itemize}

For our robot system, the generalized forces include:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{Q} = \boldsymbol{\tau} + \mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T \mathbf{T}
\end{equation}

where $\boldsymbol{\tau} = [\tau_1, \tau_2]^T$ are motor torques and $\mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T \mathbf{T}$ represents the cable tension effects.

\subsection{Kinetic Energy}

The total kinetic energy consists of rotational components from the two links:

\begin{equation}
T = \frac{1}{2} I_1 \dot{q}_1^2 + \frac{1}{2} I_2 (\dot{q}_1 + \dot{q}_2)^2
\end{equation}

where $I_1$ and $I_2$ are the moments of inertia of links 1 and 2 about their respective pivot points.

Expanding this:

\begin{equation}
T = \frac{1}{2} (I_1 + I_2) \dot{q}_1^2 + \frac{1}{2} I_2 \dot{q}_2^2 + I_2 \dot{q}_1 \dot{q}_2
\end{equation}

In matrix form:

\begin{equation}
T = \frac{1}{2} \dot{\mathbf{q}}^T \mathbf{M}(\mathbf{q}) \dot{\mathbf{q}}
\end{equation}

where the \textbf{Mass/Inertia Matrix} is:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{M}(\mathbf{q}) = \begin{bmatrix}
I_1 + I_2 & I_2 \\
I_2 & I_2
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

Note: The mass matrix is constant for this planar 2-DOF arm with point masses at the joint axes.

\subsection{Potential Energy}

The potential energy comes from gravity acting on the links. Considering the center of mass of each link:

\begin{equation}
V = m_1 g h_1 + m_2 g h_2
\end{equation}

where $h_1$ and $h_2$ are the vertical heights of the centers of mass.

For a 2-link planar arm in the vertical plane with angle $\alpha$ from horizontal:

\begin{equation}
h_1 = -\frac{l_1}{2} \sin(q_1)
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
h_2 = -l_1 \sin(q_1) - \frac{l_2}{2} \sin(q_1 + q_2)
\end{equation}

Therefore, the potential energy is:

\begin{equation}
V = -m_1 g \frac{l_1}{2} \sin(q_1) - m_2 g \left(l_1 \sin(q_1) + \frac{l_2}{2} \sin(q_1 + q_2)\right)
\end{equation}

Simplifying:

\begin{equation}
V = -\left(m_1 \frac{l_1}{2} + m_2 l_1\right) g \sin(q_1) - m_2 g \frac{l_2}{2} \sin(q_1 + q_2)
\end{equation}

\subsection{7.4 Lagrangian}

The Lagrangian is:

\begin{equation}
L = T - V = \frac{1}{2} \dot{\mathbf{q}}^T \mathbf{M} \dot{\mathbf{q}} + \left(m_1 \frac{l_1}{2} + m_2 l_1\right) g \sin(q_1) + m_2 g \frac{l_2}{2} \sin(q_1 + q_2)
\end{equation}

\subsection{Partial Derivatives for Euler-Lagrange Equation}

\subsubsection{Derivative with respect to velocities}

\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_1} = (I_1 + I_2) \dot{q}_1 + I_2 \dot{q}_2
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_2} = I_2 \dot{q}_1 + I_2 \dot{q}_2
\end{equation}

Taking time derivatives:

\begin{equation}
\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_1}\right) = (I_1 + I_2) \ddot{q}_1 + I_2 \ddot{q}_2
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_2}\right) = I_2 \ddot{q}_1 + I_2 \ddot{q}_2
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Derivatives with respect to positions}

\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial L}{\partial q_1} = \left(m_1 \frac{l_1}{2} + m_2 l_1\right) g \cos(q_1) + m_2 g \frac{l_2}{2} \cos(q_1 + q_2)
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial L}{\partial q_2} = m_2 g \frac{l_2}{2} \cos(q_1 + q_2)
\end{equation}

\subsection{Equations of Motion}

Applying the Euler-Lagrange equation for each degree of freedom:

\textbf{For joint 1:}

\begin{equation}
(I_1 + I_2) \ddot{q}_1 + I_2 \ddot{q}_2 = \tau_1 + J_{\rho1,1} T_1 + J_{\rho1,2} T_2 - \left(m_1 \frac{l_1}{2} + m_2 l_1\right) g \cos(q_1) - m_2 g \frac{l_2}{2} \cos(q_1 + q_2)
\end{equation}

\textbf{For joint 2:}

\begin{equation}
I_2 \ddot{q}_1 + I_2 \ddot{q}_2 = \tau_2 + J_{\rho2,1} T_1 + J_{\rho2,2} T_2 - m_2 g \frac{l_2}{2} \cos(q_1 + q_2)
\end{equation}

where $J_{\rho i,j}$ are the elements of the Cable Geometry Jacobian $\mathbf{J}_{\rho}$ (see Section 5.2).

\subsection{Matrix Form of Equations of Motion}

The complete equations of motion can be written in matrix form:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{\mathbf{M}(\mathbf{q}) \ddot{\mathbf{q}} + \mathbf{C}(\mathbf{q}, \dot{\mathbf{q}}) \dot{\mathbf{q}} + \mathbf{G}(\mathbf{q}) = \boldsymbol{\tau} + \mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T(\mathbf{q}) \mathbf{T}}
\end{equation}

where:

\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathbf{M}(\mathbf{q})$ is the Mass/Inertia Matrix (symmetric, positive definite)
    \item $\mathbf{C}(\mathbf{q}, \dot{\mathbf{q}})$ is the Centrifugal and Coriolis Matrix
    \item $\mathbf{G}(\mathbf{q})$ is the Gravitational Torque Vector
    \item $\boldsymbol{\tau}$ are the motor torques (actuator torques)
    \item $\mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T \mathbf{T}$ are the torques from cable tensions
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Component Matrices}

\subsubsection{Mass Matrix}

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{M}(\mathbf{q}) = \begin{bmatrix}
I_1 + I_2 & I_2 \\
I_2 & I_2
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

For this planar robot, the mass matrix is \textbf{independent of configuration} (constant). This simplification occurs because we assumed point masses at the pivots. For a more general case with distributed mass, $\mathbf{M}$ would depend on $\mathbf{q}$.

\textbf{Properties:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Symmetric: $\mathbf{M} = \mathbf{M}^T$
    \item Positive definite: $\mathbf{x}^T \mathbf{M} \mathbf{x} > 0$ for all $\mathbf{x} \neq 0$
    \item $\det(\mathbf{M}) = I_1 I_2 > 0$ (always invertible)
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Coriolis and Centrifugal Matrix}

For a system where $\mathbf{M}$ is constant, the Coriolis and centrifugal terms vanish:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{C}(\mathbf{q}, \dot{\mathbf{q}}) \dot{\mathbf{q}} = \mathbf{0}
\end{equation}

This is because:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{C}_{ij} = \sum_{k} \left(\frac{\partial M_{ij}}{\partial q_k}\right) \dot{q}_k = 0 \text{ when } \mathbf{M} \text{ is constant}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Note:} If we include distributed mass effects, non-zero Coriolis terms would appear.

\subsubsection{Gravitational Torque Vector}

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{G}(\mathbf{q}) = \begin{bmatrix}
\left(m_1 \frac{l_1}{2} + m_2 l_1\right) g \cos(q_1) + m_2 g \frac{l_2}{2} \cos(q_1 + q_2) \\
m_2 g \frac{l_2}{2} \cos(q_1 + q_2)
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

This represents the gravitational torques required to hold the arm in a given configuration.

\subsection{Cable Tension Coupling}

The cable tensions couple through the Cable Geometry Jacobian transpose:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T(\mathbf{q}) \mathbf{T} = \begin{bmatrix}
\frac{\partial \rho_1}{\partial q_1} T_1 + \frac{\partial \rho_2}{\partial q_1} T_2 \\
\frac{\partial \rho_1}{\partial q_2} T_1 + \frac{\partial \rho_2}{\partial q_2} T_2
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

where the partial derivatives are computed from the cable length constraints (Section 5.2):

\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \rho_i}{\partial q_j} = -\frac{1}{\rho_i} \frac{\partial}{\partial q_j} \left[(x_M + x_{A_i})^2 + (y_M + y_{A_i})^2\right]
\end{equation}

\subsection{Simplified Equations of Motion}

For the case where Coriolis terms are negligible and assuming $I_1 \approx I_2 = I$:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{M} \ddot{\mathbf{q}} + \mathbf{G}(\mathbf{q}) = \boldsymbol{\tau} + \mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T(\mathbf{q}) \mathbf{T}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Explicit form:}

\begin{equation}
\begin{bmatrix}
2I & I \\
I & I
\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}
\ddot{q}_1 \\
\ddot{q}_2
\end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix}
g_1(q_1, q_2) \\
g_2(q_1, q_2)
\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}
\tau_1 \\
\tau_2
\end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix}
J_{\rho 1,1} T_1 + J_{\rho 1,2} T_2 \\
J_{\rho 2,1} T_1 + J_{\rho 2,2} T_2
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Inverse Dynamics}

Given desired joint accelerations $\ddot{\mathbf{q}}_d$ and current configuration $\mathbf{q}$, $\dot{\mathbf{q}}$, we can solve for required torques:

\begin{equation}
\boldsymbol{\tau}_d = \mathbf{M}(\mathbf{q}) \ddot{\mathbf{q}}_d + \mathbf{G}(\mathbf{q}) - \mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T(\mathbf{q}) \mathbf{T}
\end{equation}

This is the \textbf{feedforward control law} that accounts for:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Inertial effects ($\mathbf{M} \ddot{\mathbf{q}}_d$)
    \item Gravity compensation ($\mathbf{G}$)
    \item Cable tension effects ($\mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T \mathbf{T}$)
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Singularity Effects on Dynamics}

The Euler-Lagrange formulation reveals critical relationships between kinematics and dynamics:

\subsubsection{Singularity Configuration}

At singularity where $\det(\mathbf{J}_{\rho}) = 0$:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Velocity mapping is rank-deficient}: Not all Cartesian velocities are achievable
    \item \textbf{Force mapping becomes problematic}: The inverse transpose relationship breaks down
    \item \textbf{Cable tensions become indeterminate}: Multiple tension combinations produce same force
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Near-Singularity Behavior}

As $\det(\mathbf{J}_{\rho}) \to 0$:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{J}_{\rho}^{-T} = \frac{1}{\det(\mathbf{J}_{\rho})} \text{adj}(\mathbf{J}_{\rho}) \to \infty
\end{equation}

This implies:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Small external forces require large cable tensions
    \item Large external forces can produce infinitesimal motions
    \item System becomes numerically unstable for control
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Acceleration Amplification}

Near singularity, the relation:

\begin{equation}
\ddot{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{J}_{\rho}(\mathbf{q}) \ddot{\mathbf{q}} + \dot{\mathbf{J}}_{\rho}(\mathbf{q}, \dot{\mathbf{q}}) \dot{\mathbf{q}}
\end{equation}

becomes ill-conditioned. Small joint accelerations can produce very large end-effector accelerations.

\subsection{Numerical Example: Dynamic Simulation}

Consider the following robot parameters:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $l_1 = 0.5$ m, $l_2 = 0.4$ m
    \item $m_1 = 2$ kg, $m_2 = 1.5$ kg
    \item $I_1 = 0.1$ kg·m², $I_2 = 0.08$ kg·m²
    \item $g = 9.81$ m/s²
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Configuration:} $\mathbf{q} = [45°, 30°]^T$, $\dot{\mathbf{q}} = [0, 0]^T$

\textbf{Step 1: Compute Mass Matrix}

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{M} = \begin{bmatrix}
0.1 + 0.08 & 0.08 \\
0.08 & 0.08
\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}
0.18 & 0.08 \\
0.08 & 0.08
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Step 2: Compute Gravitational Torques}

\begin{align}
g_1 &= (2 \times 0.25 + 1.5 \times 0.5) \times 9.81 \times \cos(45°) + 1.5 \times 9.81 \times 0.2 \times \cos(75°) \\
&= 2.2 \times 9.81 \times 0.707 + 2.94 \times 0.259 \\
&= 15.2 + 0.76 = 15.96 \text{ N·m}
\end{align}

\begin{align}
g_2 &= 1.5 \times 9.81 \times 0.2 \times \cos(75°) \\
&= 0.76 \text{ N·m}
\end{align}

\textbf{Step 3: Compute Cable Geometry Jacobian}

From Section 5.2, for cable anchors at $(±L, 0)$ with $L = 0.5$ m:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{J}_{\rho} = \begin{bmatrix}
-\frac{x_M + L}{\rho_1} & 0 \\
-\frac{x_M - L}{\rho_2} & 0 \\
-\frac{y_M}{\rho_1} & -\frac{y_M}{\rho_2}
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

For $q_1 = 45°$, $q_2 = 30°$:
$x_M = 0.5\cos(45°) + 0.4\cos(75°) = 0.354 + 0.103 = 0.457$ m
$y_M = 0.5\sin(45°) + 0.4\sin(75°) = 0.354 + 0.386 = 0.740$ m

Cable lengths:
$\rho_1 = \sqrt{(0.457+0.5)^2 + 0.740^2} = \sqrt{0.910 + 0.548} = 1.204$ m
$\rho_2 = \sqrt{(0.457-0.5)^2 + 0.740^2} = \sqrt{0.0018 + 0.548} = 0.742$ m

\textbf{Step 4: Applied Cable Tensions}

Suppose $\mathbf{T} = [15, 12]^T$ N

\textbf{Step 5: Solve for Accelerations}

Given desired feedforward torques (e.g., $\boldsymbol{\tau} = [5, 3]^T$ N·m):

\begin{equation}
\ddot{\mathbf{q}} = \mathbf{M}^{-1} \left( \boldsymbol{\tau} + \mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T \mathbf{T} - \mathbf{G}(\mathbf{q}) \right)
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{M}^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix}
0.18 & 0.08 \\
0.08 & 0.08
\end{bmatrix}^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix}
14.29 & -14.29 \\
-14.29 & 32.14
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

Computing $\mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T \mathbf{T}$ and solving yields the joint accelerations.

\textbf{Key Observations:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Near singularity ($\det(\mathbf{J}_{\rho}) \approx 0$), small changes in $\mathbf{T}$ cause large changes in $\ddot{\mathbf{q}}$
    \item Gravity dominates for certain configurations
    \item Cable tensions can either assist or resist motion depending on geometry
\end{itemize}

\subsection{7.14 Connection to Singularity Analysis}

The Euler-Lagrange formulation directly connects to singularity analysis:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Singularity Detection}: Monitor $\det(\mathbf{J}_{\rho})$ and $\det(\mathbf{M})$ during motion
    
    \item \textbf{Control Near Singularities}: 
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Use damping terms to stabilize: $\mathbf{M} \ddot{\mathbf{q}} + \mathbf{B} \dot{\mathbf{q}} + \mathbf{G} = \mathbf{Q}$
        \item Implement singular value decomposition (SVD) based controllers
        \item Limit cable tensions in singular regions
    \end{itemize}
    
    \item \textbf{Trajectory Planning}: Avoid or plan smooth passages through singularities
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Pre-compute singular surfaces in the configuration space
        \item Plan trajectories that maintain distance from singularities
        \item Use null-space motions for singularity avoidance
    \end{itemize}
    
    \item \textbf{Force Control}: Understand amplification effects
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Near singularity: $\mathbf{F}_{ext} = \mathbf{J}_{\rho}^{-T} \mathbf{T}$ becomes ill-conditioned
        \item Apply force limits to prevent damage
        \item Monitor condition number $\kappa(\mathbf{J}_{\rho})$ continuously
    \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Advanced Topics}

\subsubsection{Damping and Friction}

In practice, joint friction and cable friction must be included:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{M} \ddot{\mathbf{q}} + \mathbf{B} \dot{\mathbf{q}} + \mathbf{G}(\mathbf{q}) = \boldsymbol{\tau} + \mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T \mathbf{T}
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{B}$ is the damping matrix. This term is crucial near singularities where it provides stabilization.

\subsubsection{Constraint Forces}

When the robot interacts with the environment (contact), constraint forces must be included:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{M} \ddot{\mathbf{q}} + \mathbf{B} \dot{\mathbf{q}} + \mathbf{G}(\mathbf{q}) = \boldsymbol{\tau} + \mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T \mathbf{T} + \mathbf{J}_c^T \mathbf{F}_c
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{J}_c$ is the contact Jacobian and $\mathbf{F}_c$ is the contact force vector.

\subsubsection{Elastic Cables}

For compliant cables with stiffness $k_c$:

\begin{equation}
\dot{T}_i = k_c (\dot{\rho}_i - v_i)
\end{equation}

This introduces additional dynamics and can affect singularity behavior through cable elongation.

\subsubsection{Nonlinear Control}

Feedback linearization controller:

\begin{equation}
\boldsymbol{\tau} + \mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T \mathbf{T} = \mathbf{M}(\mathbf{q}) \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{G}(\mathbf{q})
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{u}$ is the linearized control input. This eliminates gravity and inertial coupling but requires accurate dynamic model.

\subsection{Cable-Driven Robot Simplification (Pure Cable Actuation)}

For a purely cable-driven robot without joint motors, we assume $\tau = 0$ (motor torques are zero). This simplifies the Euler-Lagrange equations significantly and is the case for our hybrid serial-cable driven planar robot.

\subsubsection{Simplified Dynamics with tau = 0}

Setting motor torques to zero in the general dynamics equation:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{M}(\mathbf{q}) \ddot{\mathbf{q}} + \mathbf{G}(\mathbf{q}) = \mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T(\mathbf{q}) \mathbf{T}
\end{equation}

Solving for joint accelerations:

\begin{equation}
\ddot{\mathbf{q}} = \mathbf{M}^{-1} \left[ \mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T(\mathbf{q}) \mathbf{T} - \mathbf{G}(\mathbf{q}) \right]
\end{equation}

This is the \textbf{fundamental equation for cable-driven robots}, showing that:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Joint accelerations result from cable tension forces (through $\mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T \mathbf{T}$)
    \item Gravity must be overcome by cable tensions
    \item Inertia couples the dynamics through the mass matrix $\mathbf{M}$
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Explicit Form for 2-DOF Cable-Driven Arm}

For our specific planar 2-DOF arm with $\boldsymbol{\tau} = \mathbf{0}$:

\begin{equation}
\begin{bmatrix}
\ddot{q}_1 \\
\ddot{q}_2
\end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{M}^{-1} \begin{bmatrix}
J_{\rho 1,1} T_1 + J_{\rho 1,2} T_2 - g_1 \\
J_{\rho 2,1} T_1 + J_{\rho 2,2} T_2 - g_2
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

where:
\begin{align}
g_1 &= \left(m_1 \frac{l_1}{2} + m_2 l_1\right) g \cos(q_1) + m_2 g \frac{l_2}{2} \cos(q_1 + q_2) \\
g_2 &= m_2 g \frac{l_2}{2} \cos(q_1 + q_2)
\end{align}

are the gravitational torques.

\subsubsection{Force Balance Equations}

Expanding the two equations explicitly:

\textbf{Joint 1 equation:}

\begin{equation}
-\dot{Z}_1 - T_1 \cos \alpha_1 - T_2 \cos \alpha_2 = 0
\end{equation}

\textbf{Joint 2 equation (Torque balance around end-effector):}

\begin{equation}
\dot{l}\sin(q_2 + \sigma) - \sigma \dot{M} = \frac{1}{2}m_1 g \cos(q_1 + q_2)
\end{equation}

where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\alpha_1, \alpha_2$ = Cable angles with respect to joint frames
    \item $\dot{Z}_1$ = Time derivative of cable length constraint
    \item $\sigma, \dot{M}$ = Angular and velocity components
\end{itemize}

These equations represent the \textbf{force and torque equilibrium} in the cable-driven system.

\subsubsection{Physical Interpretation}

The cable-driven simplification reveals:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Passive joint dynamics}: Joints move only due to cable tension and gravity
    
    \item \textbf{Tension coupling}: Cable tensions directly determine joint accelerations
    
    \item \textbf{Gravity compensation required}: Cables must provide torque to overcome gravity
    
    \item \textbf{Constraint satisfaction}: The force balance equations (equation 6 from attachment) must be satisfied at all times
    
    \item \textbf{Singularity implications}: At singularities, the force balance becomes indeterminate or impossible to satisfy
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Comparison: Motor vs. Cable Actuation}

\textbf{With motor torques} ($\boldsymbol{\tau} \neq \mathbf{0}$):
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{M}(\mathbf{q}) \ddot{\mathbf{q}} + \mathbf{G}(\mathbf{q}) = \boldsymbol{\tau} + \mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T(\mathbf{q}) \mathbf{T}
\end{equation}
- Two independent control inputs: joint torques $\boldsymbol{\tau}$
- Full controllability (in non-singular regions)
- Can overcome gravity without cable tension

\textbf{Pure cable actuation} ($\boldsymbol{\tau} = \mathbf{0}$):
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{M}(\mathbf{q}) \ddot{\mathbf{q}} + \mathbf{G}(\mathbf{q}) = \mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T(\mathbf{q}) \mathbf{T}
\end{equation}
- Only cable tensions control motion
- Constrained by cable geometry constraints
- Gravity affects workspace boundaries
- More dependent on singularity-free regions

\subsubsection{Numerical Form of Force Balance (Equation 6)}

Your attachment shows the force balance in component form:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{\begin{aligned}
-\dot{X}_1 - T_1 \cos(\alpha_1) - T_2 \cos(\alpha_2) &= 0 \\
\dot{l} \sin(\theta_2 + \sigma) - \sigma \dot{M} &= \frac{1}{2} m_1 g \cos(q_1 + q_2)
\end{aligned}}
\end{equation}

These are the \textbf{static equilibrium conditions} when accelerations are zero ($\ddot{\mathbf{q}} = \mathbf{0}$):

Setting $\ddot{\mathbf{q}} = \mathbf{0}$ in the cable-driven dynamics:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{0} = \mathbf{M}^{-1} \left[ \mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T(\mathbf{q}) \mathbf{T} - \mathbf{G}(\mathbf{q}) \right]
\end{equation}

This implies:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T(\mathbf{q}) \mathbf{T} = \mathbf{G}(\mathbf{q})
\end{equation}

\textbf{This is the equilibrium condition:} Cable tensions must exactly balance gravity torques.

Expanding component-wise:

\begin{align}
J_{\rho 1,1} T_1 + J_{\rho 1,2} T_2 &= g_1 \\
J_{\rho 2,1} T_1 + J_{\rho 2,2} T_2 &= g_2
\end{align}

Which matches the structure of equation (6) in your attachment.

\subsubsection{Singularity Effects on Force Balance}

At singularities where $\det(\mathbf{J}_{\rho}) = 0$:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Indeterminate tensions}: The inverse problem $\mathbf{T} = (\mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T)^{-1} \mathbf{G}$ has no unique solution
    
    \item \textbf{Force amplification}: Small changes in cable angles cause large changes in tensions
    
    \item \textbf{Workspace boundaries}: Singularities define forbidden regions where equilibrium cannot be maintained
    
    \item \textbf{Dynamic instability}: Near singularities, accelerations become very large for small force disturbances
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Control Strategy for Cable-Driven Systems}

With pure cable actuation and $\boldsymbol{\tau} = \mathbf{0}$:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Tension commands}: Specify desired cable tensions $\mathbf{T}_d$
    
    \item \textbf{Compute joint accelerations}:
    \begin{equation}
    \ddot{\mathbf{q}} = \mathbf{M}^{-1} \left[ \mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T(\mathbf{q}) \mathbf{T}_d - \mathbf{G}(\mathbf{q}) \right]
    \end{equation}
    
    \item \textbf{Integrate to get motion}:
    \begin{equation}
    \dot{\mathbf{q}}(t) = \dot{\mathbf{q}}(0) + \int_0^t \ddot{\mathbf{q}}(\tau) d\tau
    \end{equation}
    
    \item \textbf{Check constraints}: Ensure tensions remain positive and workspace is non-singular
    
    \item \textbf{Adapt tensions}: Adjust $\mathbf{T}_d$ based on feedback to track desired trajectory
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Summary: Cable-Driven Dynamics Simplification}

For a purely cable-driven robot with $\boldsymbol{\tau} = \mathbf{0}$ (no actuation at joints):

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{No joint actuation}: $\tau_1 = \tau_2 = 0$ - No motors, actuators, or external forces applied directly at the joints
    
    \item \textbf{Dynamics reduce to}: $\mathbf{M}(\mathbf{q}) \ddot{\mathbf{q}} + \mathbf{G}(\mathbf{q}) = \mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T(\mathbf{q}) \mathbf{T}$
    
    \item \textbf{Force balance at equilibrium}: $\mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T(\mathbf{q}) \mathbf{T} = \mathbf{G}(\mathbf{q})$ (Equation 6)
    
    \item \textbf{Pure cable control}: All actuation and positioning achieved through cable length variations only
    
    \item \textbf{Underactuation challenges}: Limited control authority compared to fully actuated systems
    
    \item \textbf{Singularities critical}: When cables cannot provide required force directions, system loses controllability
    
    \item This formulation represents the baseline case for our hybrid serial-cable driven planar robot configuration
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Wrench Analysis and Force Balance at End-Effector}

The hybrid serial-cable robot system involves multiple force and wrench contributions at each joint and at the end-effector point $M$ where cable forces are applied. This analysis provides detailed equilibrium conditions for the system.

\paragraph{Joint Configuration and Geometric Parameters}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Link lengths: $l_1, l_2$ from origin $O$ to joints and end-effector

\paragraph{Gravitational Force Distribution}

The weights of link 1 and link 2 are applied at the mass centers of each link:

\begin{equation}

\end{equation}

where $m_1, m_2$ are link masses and $g$ is gravitational acceleration.

\paragraph{Derivation of Joint Equilibrium Equations}

The equilibrium equations are derived using the principle of virtual work and free-body diagram analysis. For a system in static equilibrium, both the sum of forces and sum of moments about any point must be zero.

\subparagraph{Free-Body Diagram Approach}

Consider a planar two-link robot with:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Link 1: length $l_1$, mass $m_1$, joint angle $q_1$
    \item Link 2: length $l_2$, mass $m_2$, joint angle $q_2$  
    \item Cable forces: $T_1, T_2$ acting at end-effector $M$ (point $A$ on link 2)
    \item Applied forces: Cable forces $\mathbf{T}$ create end-effector forces $X_F, Y_F$
\end{itemize}

For static equilibrium, we apply three fundamental conditions at each joint:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item $\sum F_x = 0$ (horizontal force balance)
    \item $\sum F_y = 0$ (vertical force balance)
    \item $\sum M = 0$ (moment balance about joint)
\end{enumerate}

\subparagraph{Deriving Link 1 Equilibrium (Joint $O$)}

\textbf{Step 1: Identify all forces acting on Link 1}

Consider the free body diagram of link 1:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Weight: $W_1 = m_1 g$ (acting downward at link center, distance $\frac{l_1}{2}$ from $O$)
    \item Force from Link 2: $\mathbf{F}_{12} = [X_m, Y_m]^T$ (transmitted through the joint)
    \item Reaction force from base: $\mathbf{R}_O = [X_{r1}, Y_{r1}]^T$ (at joint $O$)
    \item Applied torque from motors/cables at $O$: $\tau_1$ (if any)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Step 2: Apply force balance equations}

For horizontal forces:

\noindent\textit{Horizontal equilibrium:}
$$\sum F_x = 0: \quad X_{r1} + X_m = 0$$

$X_m$ represents the horizontal force transmitted from link 2 through link 1 to the base. This force comes from the horizontal components of cable tensions acting on the end-effector:

$$X_m = T_1 \cos(\alpha_1) + T_2 \cos(\alpha_2)$$

Therefore: $$\boxed{X_m = T_1 \cos(\alpha_1) + T_2 \cos(\alpha_2)}$$

This is the corrected Equation (411a) including contributions from both cables.

For vertical forces:

\noindent\textit{Vertical equilibrium:}
$$\sum F_y = 0: \quad Y_{r1} + Y_m - m_1 g = 0$$

Rearranging:
$$\boxed{Y_m + Y_{r1} - m_1 g = 0}$$

This is Equation (411b). The vertical reaction force must balance the weight minus the upward component of transmitted force from link 2.

\textbf{Step 3: Apply moment balance about joint $O$}

Taking moments about the base joint $O$:
$$\sum M_O = 0$$

The moment contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Gravitational moment: $m_1 g \cdot \frac{l_1}{2} \cos(q_1)$ (acts perpendicular to link 1)
    \item Moment from force $\mathbf{F}_{12}$: perpendicular distance from $O$ to force
    \item Moment from reaction forces: perpendicular distance from $O$ times force magnitude
\end{itemize}

For force $\mathbf{F}_{12} = [X_m, Y_m]^T$ acting at distance $l_1$ along link 1:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $X_m$ contributes moment: $X_m \cdot l_1 \cos(q_1)$ (perpendicular distance)
    \item $Y_m$ contributes moment: $Y_m \cdot l_1 \sin(q_1)$ (perpendicular distance)
\end{itemize}

For reaction force $\mathbf{R}_O = [X_{r1}, Y_{r1}]^T$ at the joint:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Creates moment: $-l_1 \sin(q_1) X_{r1} + l_1 \cos(q_1) Y_{r1}$
\end{itemize}

Moment equation:
$$-l_1 \sin(q_1) X_{r1} + l_1 \cos(q_1) Y_{r1} = m_1 g \cdot \frac{l_1}{2} \cos(q_1)$$

Simplifying:
$$\boxed{-l_1 \sin(q_1) X_{r1} + l_1 \cos(q_1) Y_{r1} = \frac{1}{2} m_1 g l_1 \cos(q_1)} \quad \text{[Equation 411c]}$$

\subparagraph{Deriving Link 2 Equilibrium (Joint $A$)}

\textbf{Step 1: Identify all forces acting on Link 2}

Consider the free body diagram of link 2:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Weight: $W_2 = m_2 g$ (acting downward at link center, distance $\frac{l_2}{2}$ from joint)
    \item Cable force: $\mathbf{T} = [T_1, T_2]^T$ (but appears as force $[X_A, Y_A]$ after projection)
    \item Reaction force from link 1 at joint $A$: $\mathbf{R}_A = [X_A, Y_A]^T$ (equal and opposite to $\mathbf{F}_{12}$ by Newton's 3rd law)
    \item Internal constraint forces from cable geometry
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Sign Convention Analysis:}

From Figure \ref{fig:link2_fbd}, the sign conventions are:

    \item Cable forces $T_1, T_2$: Always positive magnitudes, directions determined by angles $\alpha_1, \alpha_2$
    \item Weight $m_2 g$: Acts downward (negative $y$-direction)
    \item Cable angles $\alpha_1, \alpha_2$: Measured from positive $x$-axis to cable direction
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Step 2: Apply force balance equations}

The cable tensions act along specific directions determined by cable geometry. **Both cables** $T_1$ and $T_2$ apply forces to the end-effector at point M. The net force components are:

For horizontal forces:
$$\sum F_x = 0: \quad X_A + T_1 \cos(\alpha_1) + T_2 \cos(\alpha_2) = 0$$

where $\alpha_1, \alpha_2$ are the cable angles relative to horizontal at the end-effector.
$$\boxed{X_A = -T_1 \cos(\alpha_1) - T_2 \cos(\alpha_2)} \quad \text{[Equation 412a - Corrected]}$$

For vertical forces:
$$\sum F_y = 0: \quad Y_A + T_1 \sin(\alpha_1) + T_2 \sin(\alpha_2) - m_2 g = 0$$

This shows the vertical force at point $A$ must balance:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The vertical components of both cable tensions: $T_1 \sin(\alpha_1) + T_2 \sin(\alpha_2)$
    \item The weight of link 2: $m_2 g$
\end{itemize}

$$\boxed{Y_A = m_2 g - T_1 \sin(\alpha_1) - T_2 \sin(\alpha_2)} \quad \text{[Equation 412b - Corrected]}$$

\textbf{Step 3: Apply moment balance about joint A}

Taking moments about joint A (connection between links 1 and 2):
$$\sum M_A = 0$$

Moment contributions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Gravitational moment}: $m_2 g \cdot \frac{l_2}{2} \cos(q_2)$ (weight acts at center of link 2)
    \item \textbf{Cable force moments}: Cable forces $T_1, T_2$ act at end-effector M, distance $l_2$ from joint A
    \item \textbf{Joint reaction forces}: $X_A, Y_A$ act at joint A (zero moment arm)
\end{itemize}

The cable forces create moments about joint A:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $T_1$ horizontal component: $T_1 \cos(\alpha_1) \cdot l_2 \sin(q_2)$ 
    \item $T_1$ vertical component: $T_1 \sin(\alpha_1) \cdot l_2 \cos(q_2)$
    \item $T_2$ horizontal component: $T_2 \cos(\alpha_2) \cdot l_2 \sin(q_2)$
    \item $T_2$ vertical component: $T_2 \sin(\alpha_2) \cdot l_2 \cos(q_2)$
\end{itemize}

The complete moment equation becomes:
$$T_1 \cos(\alpha_1) \cdot l_2 \sin(q_2) + T_1 \sin(\alpha_1) \cdot l_2 \cos(q_2) + T_2 \cos(\alpha_2) \cdot l_2 \sin(q_2) + T_2 \sin(\alpha_2) \cdot l_2 \cos(q_2) = m_2 g \cdot \frac{l_2}{2} \cos(q_2)$$

Factoring out $l_2$:
$$\boxed{l_2[T_1(\cos(\alpha_1) \sin(q_2) + \sin(\alpha_1) \cos(q_2)) + T_2(\cos(\alpha_2) \sin(q_2) + \sin(\alpha_2) \cos(q_2))] = \frac{1}{2} m_2 g l_2 \cos(q_2)} \quad \text{[Equation 412c - Corrected]}$$

Using trigonometric identity $\cos(A)\sin(B) + \sin(A)\cos(B) = \sin(A+B)$:
$$\boxed{l_2[T_1 \sin(\alpha_1 + q_2) + T_2 \sin(\alpha_2 + q_2)] = \frac{1}{2} m_2 g l_2 \cos(q_2)} \quad \text{[Equation 412c - Final]}$$

\subparagraph{General Equilibrium Framework}

With the corrected force definitions, the equilibrium equations become:

\textbf{From the force balance analysis:}
\begin{align}
X_m &= T_1 \cos(\alpha_1) + T_2 \cos(\alpha_2) && \text{[Equation 411a]} \\
X_{r1} + X_m &= 0 && \text{[Horizontal equilibrium]} \\
Y_{r1} + Y_m - m_1 g &= 0 && \text{[Vertical equilibrium]} \\
-l_1 \sin(q_1) X_{r1} + l_1 \cos(q_1) Y_{r1} &= \frac{1}{2} m_1 g l_1 \cos(q_1) && \text{[Moment equilibrium]}
\end{align}

\textbf{For Link 2 (end-effector):}
\begin{align}
X_A &= -T_1 \cos(\alpha_1) - T_2 \cos(\alpha_2) && \text{[Equation 412a]} \\
Y_A &= m_2 g - T_1 \sin(\alpha_1) - T_2 \sin(\alpha_2) && \text{[Equation 412b]} \\
l_2[T_1 \sin(\alpha_1 + q_2) + T_2 \sin(\alpha_2 + q_2)] &= \frac{1}{2} m_2 g l_2 \cos(q_2) && \text{[Equation 412c]}
\end{align}

These equations show the direct relationship between cable tensions $(T_1, T_2)$ and internal force distribution.

\paragraph{Joint Equilibrium Conditions}

For the hybrid serial-cable robot, equilibrium at each joint is established when forces and moments balance. We analyze two critical points: the base joint and the end-effector attachment.

\subparagraph{Equilibrium at Joint $O$ (Base Joint - Link 1)}

At the base joint $O$ connecting link 1 to the fixed platform, force and moment balance requires:

\begin{equation}
\begin{cases}
X_m = T_1 \cos(\alpha_1) + T_2 \cos(\alpha_2) \\
Y_m + Y_{r1} - m_1 g = 0 \\
-l_1 \sin(q_1) X_{r1} + l_1 \cos(q_1) Y_{r1} = \frac{1}{2} m_1 g l_1 \cos(q_1)
\end{cases}
\label{eq:joint_O_equilibrium}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Component Explanation:}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Equation (411a) - Horizontal force balance:}
    $$X_m = T_1 \cos(\alpha_1) + T_2 \cos(\alpha_2)$$
    
    The horizontal force transmitted from link 2 through link 1 to the base joint equals the sum of horizontal components of both cable tensions acting on the end-effector.
    
    \textbf{Physical interpretation:} Cable tensions create horizontal forces that must be transmitted through the mechanical linkage to the base support structure.
    
    \item \textbf{Equation (411b) - Vertical force balance:}
    $$Y_m + Y_{r1} - m_1 g = 0 \implies Y_{r1} = m_1 g - Y_m$$
    The vertical force at joint $O$ (reaction force $Y_{r1}$) must support the weight of link 1 ($m_1 g$) minus the upward vertical component of the cable force ($Y_m$). This shows how cable tension partially offloads gravity.
    
    \item \textbf{Equation (411c) - Moment balance about joint $O$:}
    $$-l_1 \sin(q_1) X_{r1} + l_1 \cos(q_1) Y_{r1} = \frac{1}{2} m_1 g l_1 \cos(q_1)$$
    
    This moment equation equates:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Left side:} Moment from reaction forces at the base
        \begin{itemize}
            \item $l_1 \sin(q_1)$ component multiplies $X_{r1}$ (horizontal reaction)
            \item $l_1 \cos(q_1)$ component multiplies $Y_{r1}$ (vertical reaction)
        \end{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Right side:} Gravitational moment about joint $O$
        \begin{itemize}
            \item $\frac{1}{2} m_1 g l_1$: Gravity acts at link center (distance $\frac{l_1}{2}$)
            \item $\cos(q_1)$: Moment arm projection depends on link orientation
        \end{itemize}
    \end{itemize}
    
    \textbf{Physical Interpretation:} At joint $O$, the reaction forces must create a counter-moment to balance gravity's tendency to rotate link 1. The moment arm changes with link angle $q_1$, meaning equilibrium is configuration-dependent.
\end{itemize}

\subparagraph{Equilibrium at Point $A$ (End-Effector Attachment - Link 2)}

At the end-effector attachment point $A$ where cable forces are applied to link 2, force and moment balance yields:

\begin{equation}
\begin{cases}
X_A = -T_1 \cos(\alpha_1) - T_2 \cos(\alpha_2) \\
Y_A = m_2 g - T_1 \sin(\alpha_1) - T_2 \sin(\alpha_2) \\
l_2[T_1 \sin(\alpha_1 + q_2) + T_2 \sin(\alpha_2 + q_2)] = \frac{1}{2} m_2 g l_2 \cos(q_2)
\end{cases}
\label{eq:joint_A_equilibrium}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Component Explanation:}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Equation (412a) - Horizontal force balance:}
    $$X_A = -T_1 \cos(\alpha_1) - T_2 \cos(\alpha_2)$$
    The horizontal force at point $A$ results from the horizontal components of **both** cable tensions. Cable angles $\alpha_1, \alpha_2$ are measured relative to horizontal at the end-effector position.
    
    \item \textbf{Equation (412b) - Vertical force balance:}
    $$Y_A = m_2 g - T_1 \sin(\alpha_1) - T_2 \sin(\alpha_2)$$
    The vertical force at point $A$ must balance:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item The weight of link 2: $m_2 g$ (downward)
        \item The vertical components of both cable tensions: $T_1 \sin(\alpha_1) + T_2 \sin(\alpha_2)$ (upward)
    \end{itemize}
    Both cables contribute to supporting the gravitational load, with their contributions depending on the cable geometry angles $\alpha_1, \alpha_2$.
    
    \item \textbf{Equation (412c) - Moment balance about joint A:}
    $$l_2[T_1 \sin(\alpha_1 + q_2) + T_2 \sin(\alpha_2 + q_2)] = \frac{1}{2} m_2 g l_2 \cos(q_2)$$
    
    This corrected moment balance properly accounts for cable force contributions:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Left side}: Moments from cable forces $T_1, T_2$ acting at end-effector M
        \item Cable angle terms: $(\alpha_i + q_2)$ represent total angle from horizontal to cable direction
        \item \textbf{Right side}: Gravitational moment about joint A from link 2's center of mass
        \item Joint reaction forces $X_A, Y_A$ create no moment (zero moment arm about joint A)
    \end{itemize}
    
    \textbf{Physical Interpretation:} The moment balance determines how cable forces $X_A, Y_A$ must be oriented to counteract gravity on link 2. This constrains the cable geometry and explains why certain configurations become singular.
\end{itemize}

\subparagraph{Corrected Equation (411a) - Force Transmission Through Linkage}

\textbf{Final Corrected Form:}

For the hybrid serial-cable system, equation (411a) correctly expresses:

$$X_m = T_1 \cos(\alpha_1) + T_2 \cos(\alpha_2)$$

\textbf{Physical Interpretation:}

\begin{itemize}
    \item $X_m$: Horizontal force transmitted from link 2 through link 1 to the base joint [N]
    \item This force equals the sum of horizontal components of both cable tensions acting on the end-effector
    \item The mechanical linkage transmits these cable forces to the base support structure
    \item Cable angles $\alpha_1, \alpha_2$ determine the horizontal force contributions
    \item This force must be reacted by the base joint constraint forces $X_{r1} = -X_m$
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Key Physical Insight:}

The two-link serial mechanism acts as a force transmission pathway, carrying horizontal cable forces from the end-effector to the base joint. This is fundamentally different from assuming zero horizontal force.

\textbf{Corrected Equilibrium Framework:}

\textbf{At End-Effector (Point M):}
The cable forces acting on the end-effector must balance any external forces:

\begin{equation}
\sum F_x = T_1 \cos(\alpha_1) + T_2 \cos(\alpha_2) + F_{ext,x} = 0
\end{equation}

\textbf{Closed-form solution for T2:}

From the two-cable system geometry, the moment contribution from each cable about joint 1 is:

$$\tau_1 = (x_M u_{1,y} - y_M u_{1,x}) T_1 + (x_M u_{2,y} - y_M u_{2,x}) T_2$$

where $u_{j,x}, u_{j,y}$ are the unit vector components of cable $j$ (from anchor to $M$).

Substituting into the rearranged link 1 equation and combining with the explicit $T_1$ formula, we obtain:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{T_2 = \frac{\frac{1}{2} m_1 g l_1 \cos(q_1) + l_1 \sin(q_1) X_m - l_1 \cos(q_1) Y_m - l_1 \sin(q_1) \tau_{1,\text{cable1}}}{l_1 \sin(q_1) \tau_{2,\text{lever}}}}
\label{eq:T2_closed_form}
\end{equation}

where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\tau_{1,\text{cable1}} = (x_M u_{1,y} - y_M u_{1,x}) T_1$ is the contribution from cable 1 to joint 1 moment
    \item $\tau_{2,\text{lever}} = (x_M u_{2,y} - y_M u_{2,x})$ is the lever arm of cable 2 relative to joint 1
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Alternative: Direct Jacobian Inversion for T2}

The most practical approach is to use the full $2 \times 2$ system:

$$\begin{bmatrix} J_{\rho,11}^T & J_{\rho,12}^T \\ J_{\rho,21}^T & J_{\rho,22}^T \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} T_1 \\ T_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} g_1(q_1, q_2) \\ g_2(q_1, q_2) \end{bmatrix}$$

Solve by inversion:

\begin{equation}
\begin{bmatrix} T_1 \\ T_2 \end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{\det(\mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T)} \begin{bmatrix} J_{\rho,22}^T & -J_{\rho,12}^T \\ -J_{\rho,21}^T & J_{\rho,11}^T \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} g_1 \\ g_2 \end{bmatrix}
\label{eq:T1T2_inversion}
\end{equation}

where $\det(\mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T) = J_{\rho,11}^T J_{\rho,22}^T - J_{\rho,12}^T J_{\rho,21}^T$.

This Jacobian-based solution automatically captures the coupling between $T_1$ and $T_2$ through the cable geometry.

\textbf{Step 2: General Method to Compute T2 Using Cable Geometry Jacobian}

While $T_1$ can be solved from link 2 alone, $T_2$ requires understanding how both cables couple through their geometric Jacobian. The full equilibrium is:

$$\mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T(\mathbf{q}) \mathbf{T} = \mathbf{G}(\mathbf{q})$$

where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathbf{T} = [T_1, T_2]^T$ is the cable tension vector
    \item $\mathbf{G}(\mathbf{q}) = [g_1(q_1, q_2), g_2(q_1, q_2)]^T$ is the gravity torque vector at joints 1 and 2
    \item $\mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T(\mathbf{q})$ is the $2 \times 2$ geometry Jacobian transpose mapping cable tensions to joint torques
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Construction of Geometry Jacobian Transpose:}

For each cable $j$ (where $j \in \{1,2\}$ for left and right cables anchored at $(-L, 0)$ and $(L, 0)$):

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{End-effector position:}
    $$\mathbf{p}_M = \begin{bmatrix} x_M \\ y_M \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} l_1 \cos(q_1) + l_2 \cos(q_1 + q_2) \\ l_1 \sin(q_1) + l_2 \sin(q_1 + q_2) \end{bmatrix}$$
    
    \item \textbf{Cable vector from anchor to end-effector:}
    $$\mathbf{v}_j = \mathbf{p}_M - \mathbf{A}_j$$
    where $\mathbf{A}_1 = [-L, 0]^T$ and $\mathbf{A}_2 = [L, 0]^T$.
    
    \item \textbf{Cable length and unit vector:}
    $$\rho_j = \|\mathbf{v}_j\|, \quad \mathbf{u}_j = \frac{\mathbf{v}_j}{\rho_j}$$
    
    \item \textbf{Cable force (pulling toward anchor):}
    $$\mathbf{F}_j = -T_j \mathbf{u}_j$$
    
    \item \textbf{Moment about each joint:} For joint $i$ at position $\mathbf{p}_i$, the lever arm is:
    $$\mathbf{r}_i = \mathbf{p}_M - \mathbf{p}_i$$
    The torque (scalar in 2D) from cable $j$ about joint $i$ is:
    $$\tau_{i,j} = \mathbf{r}_i \times \mathbf{F}_j = r_{i,x} F_{j,y} - r_{i,y} F_{j,x}$$
    
    Since $\mathbf{F}_j = -T_j \mathbf{u}_j$:
    $$\tau_{i,j} = -T_j (r_{i,x} u_{j,y} - r_{i,y} u_{j,x})$$
    
    \item \textbf{Jacobian entry:}
    $$J_{\rho,ij}^T = -(r_{i,x} u_{j,y} - r_{i,y} u_{j,x})$$
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Complete Jacobian Matrix:}

$$\mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T = \begin{bmatrix} J_{\rho,11}^T & J_{\rho,12}^T \\ J_{\rho,21}^T & J_{\rho,22}^T \end{bmatrix}$$

where each entry is computed from the lever arms and cable unit vectors at the configuration $\mathbf{q}$.

\textbf{Solving for Cable Tensions:}

If $\det(\mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T) \neq 0$ (non-singular):

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{T} = (\mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T)^{-1} \mathbf{G}(\mathbf{q}) = \begin{bmatrix} T_1 \\ T_2 \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

This gives both $T_1$ and $T_2$ that satisfy equilibrium at both joints simultaneously.

If $\det(\mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T) = 0$ (singular configuration):
\begin{itemize}
    \item The Jacobian is rank-deficient
    \item Multiple cable tension combinations may satisfy equilibrium (indeterminate)
    \item Alternatively, no feasible tension solution exists (infeasible)
    \item Use pseudoinverse or constrained optimization with $T_i \geq 0$ constraints
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Feasibility Check:}

After solving for $\mathbf{T}$, verify:
$$T_1 \geq 0, \quad T_2 \geq 0$$

Negative tension is physically impossible (cables cannot push). If either value is negative, the configuration is infeasible with the given anchor positions.

\textbf{Step 3: Numerical Example}

For typical parameters:

    \item $m_1 = 2$ kg, $m_2 = 1.5$ kg

End-effector position:

For the **two-cable system**, we must solve the full force balance numerically:

**Step 1:** Calculate cable angles from end-effector to anchors:

In practice, use computational tools to:

    \item Check positivity and condition number for feasibility assessment
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Cable Force Transmission and Jacobian Relationship}

Cable tensions $\mathbf{T} = [T_1, T_2]^T$ act along the cable directions at end-effector $M$. These are transmitted to joint torques through the geometry Jacobian:

\begin{equation}
\boldsymbol{\tau}_{\text{cable}} = \mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T(\mathbf{q}) \mathbf{T}
\end{equation}

For purely cable-driven actuation (no joint motors), the dynamics reduce to:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{M}(\mathbf{q}) \ddot{\mathbf{q}} + \mathbf{G}(\mathbf{q}) = \mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T(\mathbf{q}) \mathbf{T}
\end{equation}

At equilibrium ($\ddot{\mathbf{q}} = \dot{\mathbf{q}} = 0$), this becomes the force balance equation:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{J}_{\rho}^T(\mathbf{q}) \mathbf{T} = \mathbf{G}(\mathbf{q})
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Singularity Effects on Force Balance}

At kinematic singularities where $\det(\mathbf{J}_{\rho}) = 0$:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Indeterminate cable tensions}: Multiple cable tension combinations satisfy equilibrium
    \item \textbf{Loss of invertibility}: Cannot uniquely recover $\mathbf{T}$ from $\mathbf{G}(\mathbf{q})$
    \item \textbf{Force amplification}: Infinitesimal cable displacements produce finite joint motions
    \item \textbf{Instability}: Small perturbations cause rapid configuration changes
    \item \textbf{Constraint violation risk}: Cables may go slack or tension limits exceeded
\end{enumerate}

This wrench analysis demonstrates why singularities are critical failure points for cable-driven systems.
